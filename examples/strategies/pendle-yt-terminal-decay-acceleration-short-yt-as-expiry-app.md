## 1. Hypothesis

Pendle Yield Tokens (YT) are contractually obligated to expire at zero. Their price is the present value of remaining yield cash flows over a finite, known time window. As expiry approaches, the time component of that valuation collapses non-linearly — accelerating in the final 30 days in a manner structurally identical to options theta decay. Retail participants buying YT for yield speculation systematically misprice this decay, treating YT like a perpetual yield instrument rather than a wasting asset. The result: YT tokens frequently trade at implied yields materially above the actual underlying yield in the final weeks before expiry, creating a predictable overpricing that must converge to zero by a contractually fixed date. Shorting YT 30 days before expiry and holding to zero captures this guaranteed convergence. The edge is not "YT tends to fall" — it is "YT MUST reach zero, and the path is accelerating."

## 2. Structural Mechanism

### 2.1 What YT Is

When a user deposits a yield-bearing asset (e.g., stETH, aUSDC) into Pendle, the protocol splits it into:
- **PT (Principal Token):** redeemable for the underlying principal at expiry
- **YT (Yield Token):** receives all yield generated by the underlying until expiry

YT price at any moment approximates:

```
YT_price ≈ Σ [daily_yield_rate × discount_factor(t)] for t = 1 to days_remaining
```

In simplified form for short maturities:

```
YT_price ≈ underlying_APY × (days_remaining / 365) × underlying_price
```

At expiry, `days_remaining = 0`, therefore `YT_price = 0`. This is not a market opinion — it is a smart contract invariant.

### 2.2 Why Decay Accelerates

The Pendle AMM uses a custom bonding curve that embeds time decay directly into its pricing math. The curve's effective "time scalar" compresses as expiry approaches, meaning:

- At T-60 days: YT price decays ~1/60th of remaining value per day (linear approximation)
- At T-14 days: decay rate per day is materially higher as a % of remaining price
- At T-7 days: YT price is a small fraction of T-30 price; daily decay is now a large % of remaining value

This is convex decay — the rate of price decline *as a percentage of current price* increases as expiry approaches. This is the structural analog of options gamma risk for the holder.

### 2.3 The Mispricing Mechanism

Retail YT buyers are attracted by high implied APY figures displayed on Pendle's UI. A YT showing "45% APY" with 20 days remaining is not equivalent to a 45% APY perpetual position — it is a 45% APY position for 20/365 of a year, worth approximately 45% × (20/365) = ~2.5% of notional. Retail participants frequently fail to discount this correctly, particularly when:

1. The underlying protocol's APY has recently spiked (e.g., airdrop farming, points programs)
2. The YT was purchased earlier at higher days-remaining and is being held passively
3. New buyers enter late in the cycle chasing the displayed APY number

The result: YT implied yield (derived from market price) exceeds actual underlying yield, sometimes by 20-100%+. This overpricing is the excess return available to the short seller beyond the guaranteed decay.

### 2.4 Why This Is Not Already Arbed Away

- **Borrow friction:** YT tokens are non-standard assets; borrowing them requires Pendle-native mechanics or OTC arrangements, which most arb desks don't bother with for small pools
- **Pool illiquidity:** Many YT pools have thin liquidity, making large shorts impractical — but also meaning retail mispricing persists longer
- **Complexity tax:** The strategy requires understanding Pendle's split mechanism, AMM curve, and on-chain position management — most quants skip it
- **Small absolute size:** Individual pools may be $500K-$5M TVL — too small for large funds, right-sized for Zunid

## 3. Entry Rules

### 3.1 Universe Selection

Monitor all active Pendle YT markets. Filter to:

| Criterion | Threshold |
|-----------|-----------|
| Days to expiry | 20–35 days |
| YT pool liquidity (TVL) | > $200K |

| YT implied APY vs. actual underlying APY | Implied > Actual by ≥ 15% |
| Underlying protocol risk | No active exploit flags, audit status green |

### 3.2 Implied Yield Calculation

```
YT_implied_APY = (YT_market_price / underlying_price) × (365 / days_remaining)

Overpricing_ratio = YT_implied_APY / actual_underlying_APY

Entry signal: Overpricing_ratio ≥ 1.15 (YT priced at 15%+ premium to fair value)
```

Actual underlying APY sourced from: DeFiLlama yields API, protocol dashboards (Aave, Lido, EtherFi), or Pendle's own oracle feeds.

### 3.3 Position Construction

**Preferred method — Pendle AMM sell:**
1. Acquire YT tokens by minting (deposit underlying → receive PT + YT, keep YT) OR purchase YT on secondary market
2. Immediately sell YT on Pendle AMM at entry
3. This is effectively a short: you've monetized the YT value at entry and owe nothing further (YT goes to zero, you've already sold it)

**Wait — this is a LONG decay trade, not a traditional short.** Clarification:

The cleanest execution is:
- **Mint PT+YT** from underlying → **sell YT immediately** → **hold PT to maturity** (PT redeems at par)
- Net effect: you've extracted the YT's current market value upfront; PT redeems at full principal; you've "shorted" YT's future value
- This is capital-efficient because PT redemption at maturity returns your principal

**Alternative — if YT borrow exists:**
- Borrow YT → sell on AMM → buy back near zero at expiry → return borrowed YT
- Requires YT lending market (currently limited; monitor Pendle ecosystem for this)

**Alternative — synthetic via PT:**
- Buy PT at discount → hold to par redemption
- This is the PT maturity strategy (already in pipeline) — do NOT conflate

**Primary execution for this strategy: Mint-and-sell-YT, hold PT.**

### 3.4 Entry Timing

Enter when:
- T-30 to T-20 days remaining (sweet spot: T-25)
- Overpricing ratio ≥ 1.15
- Gas costs < 0.5% of position value
- No pending governance changes to the Pendle pool

## 4. Exit Rules

### 4.1 Primary Exit — Hold to Expiry

- PT redeems automatically at expiry for full underlying principal
- YT expires at zero (no action needed — value already captured at mint-and-sell)
- Collect: YT sale proceeds + PT redemption proceeds = full underlying value + YT premium captured

### 4.2 Early Exit Conditions

Exit early (unwind PT on secondary market) if:

| Trigger | Action |
|---------|--------|
| Underlying protocol exploit or depeg > 5% | Exit immediately, accept PT discount |
| Pendle protocol pause or governance emergency | Exit immediately |
| YT implied yield drops below actual yield (overpricing closes before expiry) | Partial profit take — YT already sold, evaluate PT liquidity |
| Opportunity cost: better trade available requiring same capital | Sell PT at market (accept small discount to par) |

### 4.3 What "Exit" Means Here

Because the primary structure is mint-and-sell-YT + hold-PT:
- The YT leg is already closed at entry (sold)
- The only ongoing position is PT, which has a known redemption value
- "Exiting" means selling PT on secondary market before maturity (at a small discount)
- This is low-urgency — PT is not a wasting asset

## 6. Expected Return Profile

### 6.1 Return Components

```
Gross return = YT_sale_proceeds / (underlying_deposited × YT_overpricing_ratio - 1)
             + PT_redemption_at_par - PT_market_value_at_entry
             - gas_costs
             - slippage_on_YT_sale
```

In plain terms:
- **YT premium captured:** If YT is 30% overpriced relative to fair decay value, you capture that 30% of YT's fair value as excess return
- **PT par redemption:** PT bought/minted at slight discount to par; redeems at par — small additional return (typically 0.5-2% for 25-day horizon)
- **Costs:** Gas (Arbitrum: negligible), slippage on YT sale (0.1-0.5% for liquid pools)

### 6.2 Rough Return Estimate (Hypothesis — Not Backtested)

For a YT with:
- Underlying APY: 8% (e.g., stETH)
- Days remaining: 25
- Fair YT value: 8% × (25/365) = 0.548% of notional
- Observed YT price: 0.75% of notional (37% overpriced)
- Excess return from overpricing: 0.75% - 0.548% = 0.202% of notional in 25 days
- Annualized: ~2.95% excess APY on the full notional

This seems small — but the PT redemption runs concurrently, and the strategy can be run across multiple pools simultaneously. The real question for backtesting is how frequently the 15%+ overpricing condition is met and how large the pools are.

### 7.2 Backtest Steps

**Step 1: Reconstruct YT price history**
- Pull all historical YT swap events from Pendle subgraph
- Calculate implied APY at each timestamp: `implied_APY = (YT_price / underlying_price) × (365 / days_remaining)`
- Pull actual underlying APY from DeFiLlama at same timestamps
- Calculate overpricing ratio at each point

**Step 2: Identify entry signals**
- Flag all instances where: days_remaining ∈ [20, 35] AND overpricing_ratio ≥ 1.15
- Record entry price, days remaining, pool TVL, underlying asset

**Step 3: Simulate position**
- For each entry: record YT sale proceeds (at entry price)
- Record PT value at entry (= underlying_price - YT_price, approximately)
- Record PT redemption value at expiry (= underlying_price at expiry)
- Calculate total return net of estimated gas and slippage

**Step 4: Aggregate results**
- Win rate (YT always goes to zero, so the YT leg always "wins" — the risk is PT-side)
- Distribution of excess returns from overpricing
- Frequency of entry signals per pool per expiry cycle
- Drawdown scenarios (PT discount if early exit needed)

### 7.3 Key Backtest Questions

1. How often does the 15% overpricing condition occur in the T-20 to T-35 window?
2. What is the average overpricing ratio when the condition is met?
3. How does pool liquidity affect slippage on YT sale?
4. Are there systematic patterns in which pools/assets show more overpricing? (Hypothesis: points-farming pools show more overpricing due to retail speculation)
5. What is the PT secondary market discount if early exit is needed?

### 7.4 Backtest Period

- Target: All Pendle pools that have reached expiry since Pendle V2 launch (June 2023 onward)
- Minimum: 10 completed expiry cycles across at least 3 different underlying assets
- Pendle has had dozens of pool expirations — sufficient history exists

## 10. Risks

### 10.1 Protocol Risk (HIGH CONCERN)
Pendle smart contract exploit would affect both YT and PT positions. Mitigation: position size limits, diversification across underlying assets, monitor audit status continuously.

### 10.2 Underlying Asset Risk (MEDIUM)
If the underlying yield-bearing asset depegs (e.g., stETH during a slashing event), PT redemption value falls. The YT leg is already closed at entry, so this risk is purely on the PT side. Mitigation: use only blue-chip underlying assets (stETH, aUSDC, sUSDe).

### 10.3 Liquidity Risk (MEDIUM)
YT pools can be thin. Large YT sales move the price against you, reducing the overpricing capture. Mitigation: strict volume-based position sizing (≤15% of 7-day ADV).

### 10.4 Overpricing Persistence Risk (LOW)
The overpricing could theoretically widen further before converging (retail continues buying). This doesn't affect the YT leg (already sold) but is irrelevant to the trade structure. The YT leg is closed at entry — there is no mark-to-market risk on it.

### 10.5 Gas and Execution Risk (LOW on Arbitrum)
Minting PT+YT requires an on-chain transaction. On Arbitrum, gas is negligible. On Ethereum mainnet, gas could be meaningful for small positions — use Arbitrum deployment only.

### 10.6 Opportunity Cost Risk (LOW)
Capital is locked in PT until expiry (or sold at discount). For 25-day positions, this is acceptable. Mitigation: only enter when PT secondary market has confirmed liquidity for early exit.

### 10.7 The "Already Arbed" Risk (MEDIUM)
As Pendle grows and more sophisticated participants enter, the overpricing condition may become rarer or smaller. Monitor signal frequency over time. If overpricing ratio consistently stays below 1.10, the edge has been competed away.

### 10.8 Yield Rate Change Risk (LOW-MEDIUM)
If the underlying APY drops sharply after entry, the YT was "correctly" priced at entry (high implied yield was justified). This doesn't affect the trade — YT is already sold. It does affect whether the overpricing was real or just a forward-looking yield prediction. Backtest must account for this by using realized (not forward) yields.

## 12. Open Questions for Research

Before backtesting begins, the following must be answered:

1. **Borrow market:** Does any protocol currently support YT borrowing? If yes, the strategy can be run as a true short (borrow YT → sell → buy back near zero) rather than mint-and-sell, which changes capital efficiency significantly.

2. **Pendle AMM curve math:** Obtain the exact bonding curve formula from Pendle V2 whitepaper/code to verify the non-linear decay claim and derive the precise fair value formula for overpricing calculation.

3. **Points pools:** Pendle pools tied to points/airdrop farming (e.g., EigenLayer points YT) may have fundamentally different pricing dynamics — implied yield includes speculative points value, not just cash yield. These may need a separate overpricing model or exclusion from universe.

4. **Tax treatment:** In some jurisdictions, minting PT+YT and selling YT may constitute a taxable event. Confirm with legal before go-live.

5. **Pendle V3:** Monitor for Pendle protocol upgrades that may change AMM mechanics.
